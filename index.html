Respect context

Values

Values tie people together in ways that are difficult to break, especially when they’re not made explicit. Accounting for values helps teams approach collaboration honestly and respectfully.

Questions to ask

  • What does success ("good") look like?
  • What aspects of this team stand out to newcomers?
  • What values, norms, etc. are embedded in the organization and its current technologies?
  • On what issues will people need to compromise in order for our collaboration to succeed?

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • Team members share values that align with the team’s purpose.
  • The team discusses how their work relates to more broadly held values and norms — for example, how the team’s work relates to their parent organization’s policies; approaches to risk, privacy, security, etc.
  • The team acknowledges when it seeks to challenge the values of others.
  • Team members demonstrate respect, humility, and empathy; they see collaboration as being in their self-interest; and acknowledge they can always learn something from their teammates.

Power dynamics

Power dynamics shape all relationships as well as our collective ability to imagine potential futures. Power dynamics affect what we will consider possible, and the theories of change we will pursue.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • Power is respected and critically assessed.
  • Team members understand who makes which kinds of decisions and how.
  • Team members in positions of greater power seldom exercise their power to limit debate (that is, they practice forbearance). Team members in positions of lower power are able to hold superiors to account without fear of reprisal.
  • Team members demonstrate respect for the team itself (for example, by showing up to meetings on time and ready to contribute).
  • The team works to reduce any power imbalances it might create, for example, by prioritizing product features for people who need them most (not necessarily building for the majority).

Narratives

Narratives shape how we make sense of the world. Accounting for them helps us see the difference between what is real (“observable”) and what is true (“believable”).

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team is seen as legitimate. The team’s parent organization openly supports its work.
  • The team assesses its own narratives; for example, team members might reflect on any experiences that have enabled them to participate in the collaboration, such as having access to a computer from an early age, or having had positive interactions with existing systems of power (for example, government and law enforcement).
  • The team counters polarizing or divisive narratives. For example, the myth of the “lone, genius designer” and “hero narratives.”
  • The team is respectful of work completed prior to its creation. When discussing prior failures, the team seeks to understand aspects that led to those failures (conversations do not end with “That won’t work. We’ve tried that before”).
  • The team chronicles its work in a product-related blog, “weekly ship” email, etc.

Whole persons

Collaborators must be seen as more than their contributions. Respecting people on their terms encourages more honest participation.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team’s workplaces and events account for the varying needs of people, providing things like childcare, transportation, quiet spaces for reflection, translation services, gender-neutral bathrooms, etc.
  • People are encouraged to proactively communicate about themselves; for example, team members might proactively share preferred pronouns.
  • The team values emotional labor, and team members share the burden of performing it (for example, all new hires at Automattic are required to spend three weeks on the customer support team).
  • Team members practice nonviolent communication, especially while discussing sensitive topics. Team members support one another in discussing their feelings.
  • The team works in a way that respects its members need for a healthy work-life balance.

Design research

Design research grounds teams in reality by soliciting input and feedback throughout the design process. Accounting for design research helps teams better understand stakeholder and user needs, the team’s potential for impact, etc.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team collaboratively determines its problem statements, study objectives, and/or design hypotheses; describes the desired qualifications of its research participants (for example, using behavioral personas); conducts its research; and synthesizes findings.
  • The team uses multiple research methods (for example, stakeholder and user interviews, usability testing, etc.), and solicits feedback and input from people both inside and outside of the organization.
  • The team periodically shares its research findings back with the participants whose data helped inform those findings (for example, if the team conducts interviews and creates a persona, the team might share their persona back with interviewees to ensure that it adequately captures their needs).
  • The team operationalizes respect for the privacy of its research participants.
  • The team incorporates research findings into its future design decisions.

Encourage prudent participation

Purpose

Purpose shapes the team’s collective identity, and helps collaborators clarify their intent in relation to the effort itself.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team defines a vision, mission, and goals for its work. If applicable, the team describes how its work relates to the team’s parent organization.
  • The team maintains user (behavioral) personas that describe the user needs its work will address.
  • The team collaboratively determines what work is within scope.
  • The team collaboratively determines its plans of action, and divides its work into sufficiently small chunks—ideally using SMART criteria: Units of work should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound.
  • The team identifies an empowered product owner or leader for the effort.
  • The team uses qualitative and quantitative metrics to measure its progress.

Collaborative infrastructure

Collaborative infrastructure describes, more or less, the prerequisites to collaboration: the resources, tools, and norms for get things done.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team secures the time, money, etc. it needs to accomplish its work.
  • The team shares an understanding of the methods (e.g. design methods) and methodologies (e.g. iterative development) that will guide its work.
  • The team employ myriad, semi-redundant communication, collaboration, and mediation tools, such as video conferencing tools, virtual whiteboards, collaborative document editing tools, project management tools, etc. When joining an existing team, collaborators give preference to existing tools.
  • The team defaults to the highest fidelity means of communication that works for everyone; for example, showing a preference for video calls over phone calls.
  • The team hold regular information-sharing rituals such as daily standups, weekly team meetings, co-working sessions, demo days, etc.

Decision-making processes

Design is done by way of decisions. Encouraging greater participation in decision-making processes helps teams build commitment, alignment and shared understanding.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team employs decision-making processes in which all relevant options are respectfully considered and weighed.
  • Team members show an interest in the relevant factors that the team’s design decisions must account for (e.g., operating assumptions, user needs, design principles, etc.).
  • People who are not party to the decision-making process are encouraged to ask how or why decisions were made. The team states any prerequisites for non-team members who wish to contribute (e.g., using a CONTRIBUTING.md file).
  • The team is willing and able to changing its decision-making processes in response to varying needs; for example, the team provides mechanisms for pausing and returning to decisions.
  • Team members are able to acknowledge when they don’t have enough information to make a decision; for example, by saying “I don’t know”.

Shared leadership

Shared leadership nurtures collaborators’ ability think and act for themselves. Focusing on shared leadership helps discourage “groupthink” by supporting the growth of more responsible, autonomous individuals.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • Team members have a say in how they show up on the team (e.g., their roles and responsibilities) and are comfortable communicating their boundaries, preferences, and styles.
  • Team members are given authority appropriate to what they are accountable for: they are encouraged to take initiative and exercise judgement.
  • Team members exhibit positive self esteem (that is, they live consciously; they practice self-awareness, self-responsibility, and self-assertiveness; they interact purposefully; and they maintain personal integrity), and help model this behavior for one another.
  • Team members are comfortable challenging received wisdom, and discussing the team’s practices. Team members can respectfully discuss cultural aspects that may stifle creativity, such as requiring that significant decisions pass up a chain of command (thus leaving individuals paralyzed).
  • Team members help one another find opportunities for growth. Existing leaders proactively share their power or empower others; for example, via coaching, mentorship, or sponsorship.

Share understanding

Meetings and workshops

Meetings and workshops are opportunities for the team to directly produce shared understanding.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • Large meetings and workshops are held only in response to sufficiently examined problems. Organizers prepare an agenda and/or background information, and share it with participants well in advance.
  • Anyone is able to schedule a meeting in coordination with the team leader. The team leader is not the only person who can schedule meetings. Team members demonstrate respect by keeping meetings short and to the point.
  • Organizers remind attendees about the meeting in the days or hours before it takes place. Time is given for meeting “tech checks” before participants arrive. Meetings have a designated notetaker and/or timekeeper.
  • Workshops leverage multiple modes of participation (for example, using sticky notes and dot voting in addition to a presentation), and attendees are provided the tools, skills, and literacies required for their participation.
  • Meetings are designed for remote participants.

Deliberative culture

Deliberative cultures focus on shared goals, norms, etc. (on reality) rather than the merits, egos, etc. of individual team members.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team creates, maintains, and enforces a code of conduct.
  • The team resists over-specialization among its members. Team members look for ways to involve others in normally solitary activities (for example, synthesizing research).
  • The team creates spaces where members can critically reflect on recent decisions — especially those that resulted in "failures" — and the processes by which they were made; for example, using retrospectives, critique groups, town halls, “ask me anythings” (AMAs), etc.
  • Team members are comfortable giving and taking constructive criticism.
  • The team creates deliberative spaces at regular intervals throughout the design process, rather than at the very end (“post mortem”), so that the team can act on what was discussed.

Community

Collaborations are only as sustainable as the communities they foster. Accounting for community helps a team ensure contributions in perpetuity.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team lists its contact information, and clearly indicates where to ask questions.
  • There is a place for people who represent the team’s work, both inside and outside the organization. If necessary, the team empowers one or more members to moderate forums and handle community relations.
  • The team considers what is best for its community, including things like cooperative business models and municipal ownership
  • The team defines the terms and conditions under which its work can be reused (for example, via an open source policy).
  • The team makes time for non-project work (e.g., coffee breaks, etc.) so that members can get to know one another as individuals as well as colleagues.

Tacit knowledge

Design efforts become increasingly opaque as they mature; decisions that were once broadly understood may become less so as they disappear into the rearview. Accounting for tacit knowledge encourages sustainability.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • The team attends to any lack of understanding its work or workstyle may cause (for example, by creating explainer videos or help documentation).
  • The team promotes literacy at least inasmuch as it relates to the team’s work (for example, if the team’s work depends on understanding and setting up a virtual machine, the team might document how to setup a virtual machine).
  • The team onboards everyone. For example, in part by maintaining things like a Handbook. The team establishes an offboarding process to ensure it can retain institutional knowledge.
  • The team starts and supports communities of practice around its work.

Acknowledgement

Acknowledging contributors demonstrates appreciation for how collaboration actually works — that collaboration is in everyone’s interest. Accounting for acknowledgement helps teams model a collaboration-friendly culture.

Signs suggesting greater participation

  • Team members regularly acknowledge one another’s contributions.
  • Praise is freely given, especially to team members who often work behind the scenes and non-team members who contribute to its successes. Praise is tailored to the contributor: some people might prefer public praise, while others might prefer discrete praise.
  • The team doesn’t does not designate any single member as its spokesperson or evangelist.
  • The team acknowledges, hires, and promotes people for their efforts across an array of diverse criteria rather than any single conception of value (e.g., code commits).
